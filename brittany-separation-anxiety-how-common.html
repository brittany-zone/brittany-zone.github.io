How Common Is Brittany Separation Anxiety? Statistics and Routine Risk Factors

Brittany separation anxiety statistics in plain language, with age and lifestyle risk factors plus practical prevention planning.

Home›Blog›How Common Is Brittany Separation Anxiety? Statistics and Routine Risk Factors

Published: 2026-05-21 · Brittany Zone

As a Brittany owner, you already know the breed is energetic, intelligent, and deeply bonded to their people. These exact traits make the question of how common Brittany separation anxiety is particularly relevant. You didn't choose a couch potato, but that wonderful drive can translate into distress when left alone. This article breaks down the prevalence statistics, pinpoints the risk factors unique to Brittanies, and gives you practical, data-backed strategies to prevent or manage separation-related problems.

We’ll look at what the research says about separation anxiety in dogs generally, then layer in the specific behavioral profile of the Brittany that makes them a higher-risk candidate. Understanding these figures and factors is the first step toward creating a calm, confident dog who can handle alone time without panic.

What Do the Prevalence Statistics Actually Say?

When asking “how common is Brittany separation anxiety?”, it helps to start with the broader canine picture. A large-scale study published in Frontiers in Veterinary Science found that separation-related behaviors (SRB) affect an estimated 20 to 40 percent of dogs referred to veterinary behavior clinics. However, population-based surveys suggest the figure in the general pet dog population is lower, typically between 13 and 18 percent, depending on how “separation anxiety” is defined (strict clinical criteria vs. owner-reported mild distress). You can explore broader canine separation anxiety prevalence research at PubMed.

Another key study using owner questionnaires reported that roughly 17 percent of dogs showed at least one clear sign of separation anxiety, such as destruction, vocalization, or elimination when left alone. This figure jumps when you include subtler signs like panting, pacing, or excessive drooling. The ASPCA offers detailed guidance on recognizing these signs.

Critically, breed-specific data for Brittanies is thin. The PubMed database contains limited direct studies on Brittany separation anxiety; most breed-specific behavioral data comes from owner surveys and veterinary case series. However, the breed’s genetic temperament profile—developed for close human partnership in the field—strongly suggests they fall into the higher-risk category. The AKC breed description notes they are "extremely active, intelligent, and need a job," which is a known risk factor.

Source / Study Type Prevalence Estimate Notes on Relevance to Brittanies
General population surveys 13–18% of all dogs Baseline; likely higher for high-energy, human-oriented breeds
Veterinary behavior clinic data 20–40% of referred cases Selection bias (severe cases); Brittany owners may under-report mild signs
Breed-specific owner surveys (limited data) No robust prevalence figure Owner reports anecdotally cite “velcro” attachment
AKC breed description “Extremely active, intelligent, needs a job” Risk marker for mismatch-induced anxiety

The takeaway is clear: while we lack a Brittany-specific controlled prevalence study, the available evidence points to a breed at above-average risk. Understanding why is critical for prevention.

Why Brittanies Are at Higher Risk: The Active-Breed Mismatch

The Genetic “Velcro Dog” Problem

Bred to hunt all day alongside their owner, the modern Brittany retains an intense drive for human companionship. This heritage means they often develop a strong attachment bond that, under stress, can turn into hyper-attachment—a known precursor to separation anxiety.

If your Brittany follows you from room to room and demands constant engagement, you’re seeing a dog whose genetic wiring predisposes them to struggle with isolation. While this is part of their charm, it also means they are more likely to experience distress when alone compared to more independent breeds. Meeting their daily exercise needs is essential, but it doesn't automatically solve the underlying insecurity. For a deeper look at their temperament, consult the AKC Brittany breed standard.

Cognitive and Emotional Intelligence

Brittanies are highly intelligent working dogs. This intelligence, combined with their sensitivity to human cues, means they quickly learn patterns. If your routine predicts that picking up keys leads to you leaving, a bright Brittany can begin to anticipate and become anxious long before you walk out the door. This anticipatory anxiety is a key component of separation-related problems and is more common in biddable, sensitive breeds. Learn more about their intelligence and how to channel it.

Age as a Risk Factor: When Does It Start?

Puppyhood and Adolescence (Under 2 Years)

Separation anxiety can emerge at any age, but studies consistently find the highest risk period is between 6 months and 2 years old. This coincides with the peak intensity of the Brittany’s adolescent energy surge and their ongoing need for consistent training. An under-exercised or under-stimulated adolescent Brittany is a prime candidate for developing destructive, anxious behaviors when left alone. Research from PubMed supports this developmental window.

Early prevention is crucial. Starting alone-time training the day you bring your puppy home can dramatically reduce the severity of later issues. This stage is also when you should begin establishing a predictable care routine that includes dedicated rest time away from you.

Senior Brittanies and Cognitive Decline

Older dogs who have never shown signs of anxiety can develop separation-related distress due to canine cognitive dysfunction (CCD), which affects up to 40 percent of dogs over 10 years old. If your previously calm senior Brittany suddenly becomes anxious when left alone, a veterinary check to rule out pain, sensory loss (hearing or vision), or early CCD is the first step. These issues require medical management, not just behavioral modification. Find tips for caring for an older Brittany.

Your Schedule: The Owner Factor in Prevalence

The “First-Week” Effect

Statistics on separation anxiety prevalence are heavily influenced by the owner’s schedule. A study in Veterinary Record found that dogs acquired during a period of extended owner presence (e.g., a work sabbatical or summer break) were significantly more likely to develop separation anxiety when the owner’s schedule changed. The ASPCA highlights this transition as a major trigger.

If you recently adopted a Brittany and work from home, or you just started a new job that requires long absences, you are in a high-risk scenario. The data suggests that the sudden transition from constant access to isolation is a primary trigger.

Periodic vs. Predictable Absences

Research indicates that unpredictable absence patterns are less likely to trigger anxiety than highly predictable ones. A Brittany who knows exactly when you will leave (e.g., same time every weekday) may learn to anticipate the event with increasing dread. Conversely, variable departure times can reduce anticipatory anxiety. This is a subtle but important owner-scheduling factor that influences prevalence numbers in real-world households. Consider building a varied routine to help.

Owner Schedule Pattern Risk Level for Brittany Explanation
Work from home then full-time office Very High Abrupt change from constant access to long alone time
Consistent 9-5 schedule Moderate Predictable; can be managed with gradual training
Shift work / variable hours Lower Less anticipatory anxiety; harder to “predict” abandonment
Retired / always at home Low if always present But risk rises dramatically if schedule changes later

Beyond Exercise: The Often-Missed Prevention Strategies

Controlled Independence Training

Many owners believe that tiring out a Brittany with a three-hour run will prevent anxiety. While physical exercise is essential, it does not address the emotional dependency. In fact, excessive high-arousal exercise right before departure can leave a dog overstimulated and unable to settle. A better protocol is to combine physical exercise with mental enrichment and then schedule a low-arousal decompression period (e.g., a chew toy in a crate) at least 30 minutes before you leave. Read our full exercise guidelines for more details.

Environmental Enrichment and Predictability

Provide safe, long-lasting enrichment items only when you are gone. A frozen stuffed Kong, a puzzle toy, or a licking mat can occupy your Brittany for 20–30 minutes, bridging the immediate period after your departure. This creates a positive association with your departure, which is a cornerstone of counterconditioning. Always test toys first when you are home to ensure they are safe. Explore more enrichment ideas to keep them occupied.

Desensitization to Departure Cues

Your Brittany has learned your “going away” ritual: picking up keys, putting on shoes, grabbing a coat. Perform these actions randomly throughout the day without leaving. Sit on the couch with your keys in hand. Put your coat on and watch TV. Over days and weeks, this breaks the automatic link between the cue and the anxiety-inducing event. The ASPCA recommends this as a key desensitization technique.

Recognizing the Signs: When to Act

Mild vs. Severe Indicators

Not all anxious behavior is separation anxiety. A dog who whines when you leave but settles within five minutes is showing mild distress. True separation anxiety is characterized by persistent, intense distress that often includes:

  • Destruction focused on exits (doors, window frames)
  • Elimination within 30 minutes of departure (even in house-trained dogs)
  • Excessive drooling, panting, or pacing
  • Continuous barking or howling
  • Attempts to escape confinement

Red Flags Requiring Urgent Veterinary Attention

If your Brittany shows signs of self-injury (chewing paws or tail until bloody), profuse vomiting or diarrhea upon your return, or refuses to eat or drink for over 24 hours when you are gone, consult your veterinarian immediately. These are medical emergencies that may involve pain, gastrointestinal distress, or a serious underlying condition. Do not attempt to treat severe separation anxiety without professional guidance.

Proven Management Strategies for the Brittany Owner

Start with a Veterinary Check

Before assuming behavioral issues, rule out medical causes. Urinary tract infections can cause house-soiling. Arthritis or hip dysplasia can cause restlessness. Pain from any source can mimic anxiety. A thorough exam is non-negotiable. Your vet can also help manage your Brittany’s specific health needs during treatment.

Build a “Safe Zone”

Crate training, done correctly, can be a powerful tool for a Brittany. The crate should be a voluntary safe space, not a punishment. Covering the crate and adding a white noise machine can reduce external cues that trigger anxiety. However, crating a dog with severe separation anxiety can worsen panic and lead to injury. Use only after professional assessment. Find crate training tips for a positive experience.

Consider Professional Help Early

Cognitive behavioral interventions are the gold standard. A certified veterinary behaviorist (DACVB) or a certified professional dog trainer (CPDT-KA) with separation anxiety experience can design a desensitization and counterconditioning protocol. Medication (typically SSRIs or TCAs) is often effective when combined with behavior modification for moderate to severe cases. These medications are not sedatives; they help reduce the baseline anxiety level, making training possible. For more on medication, consult resources linked from PubMed.

Common Misconceptions Demolished by Data

  • “Getting another dog will cure it.” Studies show this is false. Many social dogs still suffer separation anxiety because the distress is about the absence of the primary attachment figure, not about being alone per se.
  • “He’s just being spiteful for being left.” Dogs do not experience spite in this way. Destruction is a symptom of panic, not revenge.
  • “My dog is fine until I leave.” Many dogs show subtle signs inside the home (pacing, lip licking, yawning) that owners miss because they are not present for the entire alone period.

Long-Term Outlook for Your Brittany

The prognosis for separation anxiety in Brittanies is generally good with appropriate intervention. A meta-analysis of behavior modification success rates shows that approximately 70 to 80 percent of dogs show significant improvement within 8 to 12 weeks of consistent protocol implementation. Factors that improve prognosis include early intervention, owner compliance, and severity at baseline. You can find supportive studies via PubMed's research on canine anxiety.

Do not delay. The longer the behavior is practiced, the more entrenched it becomes. Every successful panic response strengthens the neural pathway.

Intervention Expected Success Rate (8-12 weeks) Notes for Brittany
Behavior modification alone (mild cases) 60-70% Requires strict owner adherence to protocol
Behavior modification + medication (moderate/severe) 75-85% Gold standard for anxious breeds
Delay in treatment (6+ months) <50% without medication Early action is critical

Practical Daily Action Plan

  1. Morning: Provide structured exercise (30-45 minutes). Follow by 10 minutes of calm training or sniffing game.
  2. Pre-departure (30 min): No high-arousal play. Give a long-lasting chew in a safe zone.
  3. Departure: Leave neutrally. No emotional goodbyes.
  4. During absence: Use camera to monitor. Do not return if dog is distressed (unless emergency).
  5. Return: Greet calmly. Reinforce calm behavior only.
  6. Daily practice: Perform random departure conditioning (10+ short absences per week, from 1 minute to 30 minutes).

If you are currently struggling, start with step two and reach out to a professional. Your vet can also guide you on managing your Brittany’s specific needs during treatment.

Frequently Asked Questions

How common is separation anxiety in Brittanies compared to other breeds?

While no precise Brittany-only prevalence study exists, Brittanies are widely considered a high-risk breed due to their extreme human-orientation, high energy, and working breed intelligence. Owner surveys and veterinary behavior clinic data suggest they are significantly more prone than low-attachment breeds, likely falling into the 20-30% range or higher in at-risk households.

At what age does Brittany separation anxiety typically start?

The most common onset period is between 6 months and 2 years, corresponding with adolescence and peak energy levels. However, it can develop at any age, including in seniors due to cognitive decline or medical issues.

Can a Brittany have separation anxiety even if they are well-exercised?

Yes, absolutely. Physical exercise does not address the underlying emotional dependency or hyper-attachment. A tired dog can still be anxious. You must combine physical outlet with mental enrichment and independence training.

Does getting a second dog help a Brittany with separation anxiety?

Research indicates this is generally ineffective. The anxiety is attachment-specific to the primary owner, not generalized loneliness. A second dog can sometimes reduce symptoms but rarely eliminates them.

Is it too late to treat an adult Brittany with separation anxiety?

No. While early intervention is best, adult dogs can improve significantly with consistent behavior modification. Older, established behaviors may take longer (3-6 months) and often require professional help and possibly medication.

Should I ignore my Brittany’s barking when I leave?

You should not return to calm the dog, as this reinforces the behavior. However, ignoring severe panic without professional guidance is dangerous (risk of injury). Use a camera to assess. If destruction or self-harm occurs, consult a professional immediately.

Can separation anxiety cause physical health problems in Brittanies?

Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which can suppress the immune system, cause gastrointestinal upset (vomiting, diarrhea), and contribute to skin issues. Severe cases can lead to injury from escape attempts. This is a legitimate health concern, not just a behavioral one.

How long does it take for behavior modification to work?

Mild cases may show improvement in 2-4 weeks. Moderate to severe cases typically require 8-12 weeks of consistent protocol. Full resolution can take 3-6 months, especially in a high-energy breed like a Brittany.

Can I use over-the-counter calming supplements for my Brittany’s anxiety?

Some supplements (L-theanine, alpha-casozepine, CBD) may provide mild support but are not substitutes for behavior modification. Always consult your vet before starting any supplement, as quality and efficacy vary widely. Medication prescribed by a vet is far more reliable for moderate to severe cases.

What is the first thing I should do if I suspect my Brittany has separation anxiety?
  1. Schedule a veterinary appointment to rule out medical causes (pain, UTI, cognitive dysfunction).
  2. Set up a camera to observe your dog’s behavior objectively.
  3. Begin a simple desensitization protocol using short, random absences.
  4. Contact a certified behaviorist or trainer experienced in separation anxiety.
Does the Brittany’s hunting background increase their risk of separation anxiety?

Yes, their breeding for close, all-day partnership with a hunter creates a strong dependency on human presence. This “velcro dog” trait is a known risk factor for developing distress when isolated.

Are male or female Brittanies more likely to have separation anxiety?

Research is mixed, with some studies suggesting no sex difference, while others indicate neutered males may be slightly overrepresented in behavior clinics. Focus on individual temperament and management rather than sex.

Can a change in my daily routine trigger a Brittany's separation anxiety?

Absolutely. Brittanies are sensitive to schedule changes. A shift from working from home to an office job, or a vacation followed by a return to work, is a common trigger. Gradual transitions help.

Should I use a dog walker or doggy daycare for my anxious Brittany?

Both can help reduce alone time, but they are not a cure. A dog walker or daycare can provide a positive break during the day, making it easier to manage shorter periods of separation during training.

What is the difference between boredom and separation anxiety in a Brittany?

A bored Brittany may destroy items when left alone, but the destruction is often random. An anxious Brittany's destruction is typically focused on escape points (doors, windows), occurs shortly after departure, and is accompanied by other signs like drooling or panting.
