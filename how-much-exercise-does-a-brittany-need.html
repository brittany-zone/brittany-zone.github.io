Why Most Brittany Owners Exercise Their Dogs Wrong

How much exercise does a Brittany need each day? Learn daily ranges by age, what counts as enough work, and the mistakes that leave this breed frustrated.

Home›Blog›How Much Exercise Does a Brittany Need Each Day?

Published: 2026-05-21 · Brittany Zone

If you are wondering how much exercise does a Brittany need, the short answer is that an adult Brittany requires at least 60 to 90 minutes of vigorous physical activity every single day, plus a separate session of structured mental work, or you will likely see a frustrated, destructive dog. This breed was developed as a tireless upland bird dog, and their daily routine must reflect that heritage, or you will both be miserable.

Quick answer: A healthy adult Brittany needs a minimum of one hour of high-intensity exercise (running, swimming, or intense retrieving) daily, plus 20 to 30 minutes of focused mental stimulation like training or scent games. Puppies and seniors have different needs, which are detailed below.

Many owners underestimate the sheer stamina of this breed, mistaking a long walk for the vigorous work a Brittany craves. A walk, even an hour-long one, will not cut it. Your Brittany needs to move with purpose. Let’s break down exactly what that looks like, day by day and age by age.

The Physical Requirement: Why Jogging Won’t Cut It

A Brittany is not a jogging partner first; they are a field dog. Their exercise should mimic the patterns of a hunting dog: bursts of speed, direction changes, and full-body effort. Unless you are covering three to five miles at a sustained trot, a leash walk is mostly sniffing time, not real exercise. The Brittany’s body is built for distance and speed, and their mind is built for problem-solving on the move.

If you cannot provide off-leash running in a safe, fenced area or a long-line field at least four times a week, you will need to substitute with structured activities like intensive fetch (using a Chuckit! or similar launcher for 20+ minutes of full sprints), swimming, or flirt pole work. These activities engage their natural gait and effort level. Without this, the dog will find an outlet, often through barking, chewing, or escaping the yard.

Age-Based Exercise Ranges

The amount of exercise your Brittany needs changes dramatically over their lifetime. Ignoring these shifts can lead to joint problems in puppies or a bored, anxious adult. The table below provides a clear guide, but always consider your individual dog’s energy level and recovery.

Age Range Daily Physical Activity Mental Stimulation Key Consideration
8 weeks to 6 months 5 minutes per month of age, 2–3 times daily (e.g., 15 minutes x 3 for a 3-month-old) 15–20 mins of puppy training and puzzle toys No forced running or jumping. Focus on socialization and short play.
6 months to 18 months 60–90 minutes of structured play, fetch, and short runs 30 minutes of obedience or scent work Growth plates are still open. Avoid repetitive high-impact exercise.
18 months to 7 years 60–90 minutes of intense work (field, swimming, bikejoring) 30–45 minutes of advanced training or dog sports This is the peak. Without this, behavioral issues are almost guaranteed.
7 years and older 45–60 minutes of lower-impact activity (walking, swimming, free play) 20 minutes of mental puzzles or nose work Watch for stiffness, arthritis, or fatigue. Adjust duration, not consistency.

Behavioral Red Flags: When Your Brittany Is Under-Exercised

An under-exercised Brittany does not just look sad; they become a problem-solving machine aimed at your belongings. Recognizing these signs early can save your sofa and your sanity. If you see a pattern of these behaviors, increase physical output or mental challenge immediately.

  • Obsessive circling or pacing: This is a common displacement behavior when a dog has unused energy.
  • Excessive barking at nothing: Especially if they are staring out the window, they are looking for a job to do.
  • Nipping or mouthing hands and sleeves: This is often an attempt to engage you in play because they are bored.
  • Digging holes or destroying flower beds: They are trying to release energy and satisfy their natural prey drive.
  • Counter surfing or opening cabinets: A bored Brittany is a clever Brittany. They are seeking stimulation.
  • Hypervigilance: If your dog cannot settle and watches every movement in the house, they are not tired.

If you notice any of these behaviors combined with a sudden decrease in appetite, vomiting, or limping, a vet visit is urgent, as these could signal a medical issue rather than a behavioral one.

Mental Work: The Non-Negotiable Half of the Equation

Physical exercise tires the body. Mental work exhausts the mind. A Brittany can run for two hours and be ready to go again after a ten-minute rest. However, twenty minutes of structured nose work or advanced obedience will leave them content for hours. This is because the breed was selected for independent problem-solving in the field, not just stamina.

Incorporate at least one 20- to 30-minute session of focused mental work daily. This could be:
- Teaching a new trick using shaping
- Hiding treats around the house for a “find it” game
- Practicing impulse control exercises like “leave it” or “stay” with distractions
- Running a short agility sequence in the yard

A great resource for structuring an exercise routine that includes both physical and mental components is the AKC’s guide on exercising with your dog, which provides practical tips for balancing both aspects.

Practical Daily Routines That Work

Rather than a rigid schedule, build a routine that packs in intensity efficiently. Most owners find success with a morning session that burns the edge off and an evening session that reinforces calmness. Here are two example routines for an adult Brittany that meet the exercise requirement.

Routine A: The Active Commuter

  • Morning (45 minutes): 15-minute walk for elimination, followed by 30 minutes of off-leash fetch in a fenced area or 20 minutes of bikejoring (if the dog is fit and trained for it).
  • Evening (45 minutes): 20 minutes of structured mental work (obedience or scent play), followed by a 25-minute sniffy walk. The walk is for decompression, not exercise.

Routine B: The Weekend Warrior

  • Weekdays (30 minutes total): 20-minute high-intensity fetch or flirt pole, followed by 10 minutes of crate games and impulse control. This is the minimum to avoid destruction.
  • Weekends (2+ hours): One long session of hiking, running in a field, or swimming. This provides the deep work the breed needs to be emotionally stable.

Common Mistakes Owners Make

Even well-meaning owners often fall into these traps, leaving their Brittany under-exercised and frustrated. Avoid them to keep your dog happy.

  • Mistaking a long walk for intense exercise: A Brittany needs heart-rate-raising activity. A mile-long walk is a warm-up, not a workout.
  • Only exercising on weekends: Brittanys do not bank energy. Skipping two days creates a buildup that explodes on day three.
  • Ignoring mental stimulation: You cannot out-run a Brittany. Physical activity alone will not tire them enough.
  • Doing too much too young: Puppies need controlled, age-appropriate exercise. Forced running on pavement damages growing joints. Let them set the pace.
  • Assuming a fenced yard is enough: Most Brittanys will not exercise themselves in a yard. They need a human to engage them.

When to See a Vet

While exercise is vital, pay attention to your dog’s signals. If your Brittany suddenly refuses to run, slows down dramatically, or shows stiffness after activity, do not push them. These can be signs of injury, arthritis, or more serious conditions like hip dysplasia or Lyme disease. A sudden change in exercise tolerance always warrants a veterinary check. If your dog is limping, crying out when moving, or showing a reduced appetite alongside lethargy, seek veterinary advice promptly.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a Brittany get enough exercise from a treadmill?

A treadmill can be a supplementary tool but should never replace outdoor work. Brittanys need varied terrain, directional changes, and mental engagement from the environment. Use a treadmill only for fitness maintenance when weather is dangerous, not as a primary exercise source.

Is two hours of walking enough for my Brittany?

Two hours of walking is likely too much low-intensity work and not enough high-intensity work. A Brittany needs at least 30–45 minutes of running or structured activity within that time frame. A long walk alone will not satisfy their breed-specific needs.

What happens if I miss a day of exercise?

Missing one day is usually fine for an adult. Missing two consecutive days often leads to hyperactivity, destructive chewing, or excessive barking on the third day. If you miss three days, expect a neurotic dog that needs a long, structured session to reset.

Can my Brittany be a good running partner for marathons?

Yes, but only after they are 18 months old and fully grown. Build distance slowly over three months. Brittanys make excellent marathon partners for up to 10 miles, but they are better suited to interval work than sustained pace runs. Watch for overheating and paw pad wear.

Do Brittanys need more exercise in winter?

No, but they need the same amount. Many owners exercise less in bad weather, which causes indoor behavioral issues. Use mental games, indoor fetch, and shorter, more intense outdoor sessions to maintain their routine. A tired Brittany is a quiet Brittany, regardless of the season.

Is a Brittany a good fit for an apartment with a dog park?

Only if you are exceptionally committed. A dog park alone will not provide enough structure or intensity. You would need to supplement with two daily high-intensity sessions and significant mental work. Living in an apartment with a Brittany requires a very active owner who prioritizes off-leash access to fields.

How do I exercise my Brittany if I have a physical limitation?

Consider hiring a dog walker who can run your dog, using a doggy daycare with structured play, or investing in a bike attachment (like a Walky Dog) if you can ride. If you cannot physically meet their needs, you must find a reliable substitute. A bored Brittany is a problematic pet.

Why does my Brittany still seem hyper after an hour of fetch?

You may be seeing overstimulation, not leftover energy. If your dog cannot settle after intense exercise, they might be overtired or the fetch session is too high-arousal. End fetch sessions with a ten-minute decompression walk or a calming chew to teach them to switch off. Breed-specific information can be found at the AKC Brittany breed page.
